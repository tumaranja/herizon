Website structure

Simple 1-page website structure for early-stage teams. It's based on conversion optimization principles and works with any design direction.

Header

Logo + one CTA. Nothing else.

Purpose

Identify the brand. Make the most-wanted action visible at every scroll position.

Conversion rulesTrust signals

CTA shows GTM strategy

Remove navigation distraction on conversion pages. The CTA shape itself signals the GTM motion to every visitor — a self-serve "Start free" vs sales-led "Get a demo" tells the buyer what kind of company you are before they read a word.

If you removed it

Visitors who scroll past the hero lose access to the primary action mid-page. Conversion drops without a sticky header reinforcing it.

02

Hero (above the fold)

WHO, WHAT, WHY — in that order, in 5 seconds.

Purpose

Convert the message-matched visitor immediately. For everyone else, set up the body content.

Conversion rules

The 5-second test

The 5 essential elements all live here: headline, subhead, hero visual, benefits implied, single prominent CTA. If a visitor can't tell who it's for, what it does, and why to believe you within 5 seconds, every other section is wasted.

Conversion rules

Activation, not aspiration

The PLG rule: show the product immediately. The hero visual is product video > product UI screenshot > illustration > stock imagery. A gradient block or abstract shape is a tell that the product asset is missing.

Conversion rules

Message match

The H1 must echo the highest-intent ICP's actual language. If the visitor searched "AI agents for non-technical teams," the H1 echoes that exact frame. Verbatim if possible.

If you removed it

There is no removing the hero. This section IS the page for ≈50% of visitors who never scroll.

Customer quote / scenario in their own words
03

Problem / pain

The "yes, that's me" moment.

Purpose

Earn the visitor's continued attention by naming their pain in their language, before selling them anything.

Conversion rules

Builds relevance + motivation

A visitor who feels understood scores higher on Motivation (m) in the conversion equation C = 4m + 3v + 2(i-f) - 2a. Motivation has the highest weight in the equation. Specificity > abstraction — name the role (bookkeeper, lawyer, ops lead), not the segment.

Use the customer's actual words

A real quote with attribution beats invented copy by an order of magnitude. If no quote exists, fall back to a specific scenario — never invent a quote.

If you removed it

The hero pitch lands in a vacuum. Visitors evaluate the solution before they've felt the problem, which feels like being sold to. The whole page reads pushier.

04

How it works

Three steps. Telegraph the magic moment.

Purpose

Show the path from problem to solution. Reduce anxiety by making the product feel knowable.

Conversion rules

Activation moment, made visible

The "aha" moment must be telegraphed here. For Pine: "First agent live in 15 minutes" + "review before anything goes out" = activation + safety in two lines. Without this, the visitor can't picture themselves succeeding with the product.

Conversion rules

Three steps, no more

More than three steps signals complexity. The visitor's brain rounds up: "3 steps = simple, 5 steps = a process, 7 steps = a project." Three is the sweet spot for "I can do this."

If you removed it

Visitors don't know how to picture using the product. Anxiety stays high (will I be able to figure this out?), motivation stays low (is the magic moment within reach?).

05

Use cases / templates

Concrete outcomes. With numbers.

Purpose

Turn "interesting" into "I can use this for X by Tuesday." Specificity is the conversion lever here.

Conversion rules

Specificity over abstraction

"Save 3 hours a day on email" beats "boost productivity." Numbers anchor value; verbs without numbers float away. Every card should pair one use case with one concrete outcome.

Conversion rules

Show the breadth of the addressable surface

For PLG, every additional plausible use case = a larger surface for self-select. Show 3-6 cards; more dilutes, fewer narrows.

If you removed it

The product feels abstract. Visitors leave saying "looks interesting" but can't articulate what they'd actually do with it on Monday morning.

06

Proof

The single highest trust lever at this stage.

Purpose

Convert "interesting" into "trustworthy." Address every anxiety the visitor has been building since the hero.

Conversion rules

Specific numbers beat soft claims

"35 teams in our first month" beats "trusted by hundreds." "15 minutes to first agent" beats "fast setup." The visitor's brain rounds soft claims to zero; numbers stick.

If you removed it

Anxiety stays high. Conversion drops sharply because the visitor has no answer to "why should I trust this?" and clicking the CTA feels like a leap.

07

Founder story

Why this team, why now.

Purpose

Address the emotional trust question — "are these the right people to bet on?" — while the visitor is still in trust-building mode after seeing testimonials. Where Proof shows that others trust this product, Founder shows who built it and why.

Trust signals

Founder-as-channel

For early-stage B2B (< Series B), the founder photo + 1–2 line quote is often the strongest single trust signal — stronger than logos, stronger than metrics. Customers buy from people they believe; investors back people they trust. That's why it gets its own section, not a card buried in Proof.

Conversion rules

Trusted by → built by: one continuous trust block

Proof and Founder belong adjacent. "Trusted by these people → built by these people" reads as one continuous trust block: external validation followed by the internal authenticity that earned it. Breaking that thread with an Objections section in between weakens both halves. Front-load the emotional content; let FAQ act as the cooler, rational checkpoint right before the commit.

When to skip

Past Series B. Once the company has scale and named customers, case studies and logos do more work than the founder. Also skip if the founder is uncomfortable on camera — a stiff or forced founder section harms trust more than no founder section.

08

Objections answered

The last checkpoint before commit.

Purpose

Eliminate the rational anxieties that prevent conversion — directly before the price reveal. FAQ sits here so every doubt cleared is a doubt that won't surface at the price moment.

Conversion rules

Anxiety is the −2a in the conversion equation

C = 4m + 3v + 2(i-f) - 2a. Anxiety subtracts from conversion. Unanswered objections = elevated anxiety = lost conversions. The objections answered here should be the top-3 from sales calls — never invented.

Why adjacent to Pricing, not Proof

FAQ is the cool, analytical beat — answers in lists, specifics, plain language. Placing it immediately before Pricing means the visitor arrives at the number with rational concerns settled. Placing it earlier (between Proof and Founder) would interrupt the warm "trusted by → built by" trust block and dilute its narrative power.

Plain, specific answers

"Is it secure? Yes!" doesn't answer anything. "SOC 2 Type II, EU data residency, one-click delete" does. A real answer cites specifics the skeptic would believe.

If you removed it

The page asks the visitor to make a leap of faith on issues they've been quietly worrying about. They don't ask; they leave.

09

Pricing

Show the numbers. Trust through transparency.

Purpose

Build trust through transparency. Filter unqualified buyers. Anchor the value of what's just been described.

Trust signals

Pricing transparency = trust signal

Hidden pricing on a PLG- or hybrid-shaped page is the most common trust killer. Default to showing prices. Hide only when the GTM is true enterprise — and explain why in one line ("Pricing depends on volume — let's scope a fit").

Conversion rules

One CTA per tier, matching the motion

"Start free" for self-serve. "Talk to us" for enterprise. The CTA shape per tier reinforces the GTM signal from the hero — and the visitor's brain matches "this tier is for people like me" by the button it shows.

If you removed it

Visitors who'd have bought self-serve drop off because they can't tell if Pine is in their budget. Investors and large buyers also penalize hidden pricing as "early-stage flakiness."

Primary CTA
10

Final CTA

The last button. Fresh framing.

Purpose

Last chance to convert the visitor who scrolled the whole page. Restate the transformation. One big button. Nothing else competing.

Conversion rules

Re-anchor the activation moment

Don't say "Sign up." Say "Start your first agent in 15 minutes." Fresh framing of the same action. The visitor who's scrolled here has been convinced — but they need one more nudge to act.

If you removed it

Visitors who finished the page have nowhere to land. They scroll back up looking for the hero CTA, lose momentum, leave.

11

Footer (utility)

Minimal. No nav menu.

Purpose

Utility only: brand mark, legal links, contact. Not a nav menu. Conversion-focused pages don't end with "here's everywhere else you could go."

Conversion rules

The "no-nav" rule

Every nav link in a footer is a path away from the conversion. Keep only what's legally required (privacy, terms) and the contact link. Social links optional.
